A Measles Resurgence?

Original Article by Teddy Rosenbluth, NYTimes

Remix by Alyssa Luong

Starting in 2025, a growing measles outbreak in Texas and New Mexico raised concerns among public health officials. Reporting from The New York Times warned that declining vaccination rates and rising skepticism toward vaccines could allow measles to return as a major public health threat in the United States.

Measles is an extremely contagious virus--one of the most contagious in recent history. In populations with no immunity, a single infected person can transmit the virus to up to 9 out of 10 people. Due to this extreme transmissibility, its estimated that roughly 95% of the community must be vaccinated to establish/maintain herd immunity. And so far, vaccination has been the single most effective tool in reducing measles infections in the United States. However, when national data is examined more closely, the picture becomes more complex than a simple narrative of widespread resurgence.

1. Measles Cases Over Time

Derailing this virus took decades of public health initiatives. Before vaccination was widespread, nearly all children before the age of 15 contracted the virus. After the measles vaccine was introduced in 1963 (which would later be combined into the MMR vaccine in 1971), it was quickly administered across the country. Over the following decades, vaccination campaigns were successful in reducing measles cases nationwide. By the early 2000s, the number of cases had dropped so low that the United States declared measles eliminated. Any of the cases that did occur after were mostly linked to international travel rather than domestic spread.

However, recent outbreaks have raised concerns that declining vaccination rates could allow measles to reemerge. The outbreak in Texas highlighted in the reporting by Teddy Rosenbluth illustrates how quickly the virus can spread when vaccination coverage drops below the level needed for herd immunity.

But even in years with notable outbreaks, modern case counts remain dramatically lower than the levels seen before widespread vaccination (which would be in the tens of thousands). This long-term trend highlights how vaccination programs have fundamentally changed the trajectory of the disease in the United States.

After examining how measles cases have changed over time, it becomes clear that not all regions are affected equally. 3. Vaccination Rates and Measles Cases (2025)

State-level vaccination rates from 2024-2025 versus cases. Dot size represents total cases. Hover to explore each state.

The relationship between vaccination rates and measles cases reinforces the critical role vaccines play in preventing outbreaks. States that maintain coverage above the herd immunity threshold (~95%) generally report very few cases. States that fall below this threshold, like Texas and South Carolina, are at higher risk of outbreaks, and indeed saw more cases in 2025. However, not all states with lower vaccination rates experienced large outbreaks. Some areas with coverage below 95% reported relatively few cases (ex: Wisconsin). This suggests that additional factors like population density, travel patterns, and local clusters of unvaccinated individuals, also influence the severity of outbreaks. By examining this relationship, we can see that while vaccines are essential for preventing measles, the size and spread of the outbreaks depend on both vaccination coverage and other local conditions.

4. Takeaways

Measles was once nearly eliminated in the United States thanks to widespread vaccination, and recent resurgence highlights how fragile that progress can be. While declining vaccination rates are a major factor driving outbreaks, broader national data shows that cases remain geographically concentrated rather than widespread. This emphasizes that vaccines are essential, but effective public health responses, such as rapid case identification, contact tracing, and isolation of infected patients, also play a critical role in controlling outbreaks. Maintaining high vaccination coverage across all communities, alongside coordinated public health strategies, remains the most effective way to prevent future outbreaks and protect public health overall.
